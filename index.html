How did you apply (or not) iterative design in your project?

By complying with the very basic requirements and delivering a “usable” product every set period of time. After that, we’d look for feedback from different sources and apply corrections or add more things. This makes the product usable even though not all things have been added.


Describe the main highlights of your persona. Explain the audience of your project.

Our audience is people from their teens to adulthood, especially the ones that use a motorized vehicle to get from place to place and are negatively affected by traffic in a metropolitan city

  1. Our persona is centered on a young mother who lives in a big city (Guadalajara), has a husband, two young kids and runs a furniture shop. She is high-class and she works for it. She drives a SUV everyday to a plaza (where the shop is located) that is not near home (takes her nearly an hour to get there on average).
  2. Her motivations are her children, business and family and friends.
  3. Perfectionist, busy, and persistent. Can get very desperate at times (especially when she is not doing anything productive; AKA stuck in traffic).
  4. Expertise:
    1. Knows and uses key roads to avoid traffic congestion. (2/4)
    2. Avoids driving during peak hours. (1/4)
    3. Drives efficiently without breaking any laws. (4/4)
    4. Remains calm even when stuck on traffic for long periods of time. (1/4)
    5. Uses a bike to get to near places. (0/4)


Explain, in your own words, the three principles of system design.

  1. Early focus on users and tasks: The design of the product should be centered on the end-user as they will be the consumer. The scope, functionality and complexity of the product should always be envisioned with how enjoyable it will be for the user. They need to be part of the design team (at least indirectly) through interviews and tests.
  2. Empirical measurement: When the user is testing something related to your product, you need to pay attention to their reactions and comments (especially those that come out naturally) as they will give you the most honest feedback. This measurements are then later used for corrections.
  3. Iterative Design: By combining the user feedback, you should be constantly correcting your design. After that, you make a prototype again and get it tested. This is repeated as much as it is needed until the product reaches the specified requirements for release (and even beyond this step).


Create your own methodology to develop a website. It’s important to justify all the steps.

  1. Define a proper style that catches the eye of potential customers.
  2. Create a service proposal that is viewable from an existing website (that is also yours).
  3. Ask the interested customer for their information.
  4. Get in touch with them and elicit their requirements. Define an estimation of all deliverables so everyone is in the same page. Discuss about initial and final costs.
  5. Continuously deliver advances and get feedback from the client. Make corrections when necessary (iterative approach).
  6. Launch product when it meets the client’s requirements and give maintenance for a set period of time.

Is your project responsive? Explain.

Mostly, yes. When the screen is small enough, the navigation bar becomes a collapsible hamburger menu with all the pages on our site. Some of the content may be hindered by tables or other sectioning tools, but text and other necessary media are good on many devices.


In your project did you follow the first three laws of simplicity? How?

Yes

  1. Reduce:
    The information in each tab is summarized, written in simple (yet formal) language so you can read it comfortably. Larger information is kept separately: collapsibles or external documents.
  2. Organize:
    We put relevant information on different sections “tabs” so the user instantly knows what they are looking at. This keeps the pages cleaner and more viewer-friendly.
  3. Time:
    The information in our page is efficiently summarized so you can get a grip of the problem without reading too much. Keeping the menus in tabs or hamburger collapsibles also makes navigation very fast.
